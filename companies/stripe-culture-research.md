# Stripe Culture, Principles, and Behavioral Interview Research

Compiled from stripe.com, Patrick/John Collison interviews, Stripe alumni blog posts, and interview candidate reports. All quotes are verbatim from their original sources unless explicitly noted.

---

## 1. Stripe's Operating Principles (from stripe.com/jobs/culture)

Stripe recently refined their principles from 16 to 6 headline principles, but the original 16 remain deeply integral and are still what behavioral interviews are evaluated against. The 16 are organized in three groups.

### How We Work (5 principles)

**1. Users first.**
> "We serve millions of businesses and a meaningful fraction of global GDP. We have a weighty responsibility to those businesses and to society. We work backwards from our users' needs, indexing especially on feedback from the most innovative. Everyone at Stripe talks to users."

**2. Move with urgency and focus.**
> "We move with speed on what matters most and take the time to invest in what will make us faster tomorrow. Whereas many companies get slower until they're eventually mired in mediocrity, we aspire to become the world's fastest company."

**3. Be meticulous in your craft.** (Also expressed as "Create with craft and beauty.")
> "With careful thought, anything can be made surprisingly great. Across every part of Stripe, we are committed to doing so. Well-crafted work indicates care for the user, and beautiful work indicates care for the world."

**4. Seek feedback.**
> "We value intellectual honesty and look for other Stripes with the expertise to refine our ideas, challenge us, and deepen our understanding across the business."

**5. Deliver outstanding results.**
> "Stripes are high achievers with a drive to succeed. We take end-to-end accountability for seeing our work through and delivering on our commitments."

### Who We Are (5 principles)

**6. Curious.**
> "We lead with a genuine interest in people, ideas, and the unknown. We work hard to understand other points of view, and prefer investigating to being right."

Longer form from the culture page:
> "Stripe is an applied exercise in learning about how businesses tick and how the world works. Since the details and possibilities are infinite, we are always seeking to learn. We're energized by the unfamiliar, preferring the joy of discovery to the comfort of certainty."

**7. Resilient.**
> "Startups are tumultuous places and the audacity of our goals will mean occasional failure. We view setbacks as opportunities to sharpen our skills."

**8. Humble.**
> "Stripes are humble, not arrogant or complacent, and create an inclusive environment for all. We aren't wedded to how we currently do things — lots about our current practices will turn out to be wrong."

**9. Macro-optimistic.**
> "Stripes reject cynicism, knowing that all problems can be solved with the right understanding, and that progress is only inevitable through focused effort."

Paired with "micro-pessimistic" in practice (see Section 6).

**10. Exothermic.**
> "Stripes generate an energy and warmth that is infectious across teams and throughout the company. We are genuinely excited about our work, and about creating an exceptionally welcoming environment for all Stripes."

### And Leaders (6 principles)

**11. Obsess over talent.**
> "It's every Stripe's responsibility to help hire the best and to push for excellence everywhere. Managers must relentlessly uphold our high talent bar, lead their teams with clarity and context, foster development, and ultimately support Stripes in accomplishing the best work of their careers."

**12. Elevate ambitions.**
> Develop compelling team visions; create energizing, rewarding goals.

**13. Set the pace and energy.**
> "Stripe is intense — we have broad ambitions and serious obligations to our users. Sign your team up for bold goals, articulate a plan to achieve them faster than expected, and focus on making this energizing, rewarding, and fulfilling for your teams."

**14. Make decisions; be accountable.**
> "It's not always clear who should make a decision. Effective leaders embrace decision-making in cases of murky ownership, either making the call themselves or collaborating across the necessary teams to drive an outcome. They clearly communicate decisions and hold themselves and their teams accountable for results."

**15. Lead with clarity and context.**
> Translate chaos into a clear, compelling plan.

**16. Solve problems.**
> Be a persistent force for progress.

### The 6 Headline Principles (condensed from the 16)

| Principle | Maps To |
|---|---|
| Users first | #1 |
| Move with urgency and focus | #2 |
| Be meticulous in your craft | #3 |
| Seek feedback | #4 |
| Deliver outstanding results | #5 |
| Curious | #6 |

### Iconic Shorthand Phrases

- **"We haven't won yet"** — Complacency is the enemy. Every shipped email ends with this section.
- **"Disagree and commit"** — Debate hard, then align fully once a decision is made.
- **"Be politely relentless"** — Persist on the right problems, but with kindness and respect. You can be intense without being a jerk.
- **"Really, really, really care"** — About users, about craft, about getting the details right.

## 2. Stripe's Compatibility Page (stripe.com/jobs/compatibility)

The compatibility page, titled "Assess your alignment," asks candidates to self-evaluate across four dimensions:

### Trait 1: Motivated by Our Mission

Stripe's mission is to grow the GDP of the internet by building tools that democratize online commerce and expand economic opportunity.

Self-assessment questions:
- "Do I see free markets and entrepreneurship as powerful forces for advancing progress?"
- "Is helping to unleash the internet economy's full potential the highest use of my time and talents?"

### Trait 2: Embody Our Operating Principles

Stripe users depend on reliable service delivery. "Users first" requires flexibility across time zones. The culture favors "how might this work?" over obstacle-focused thinking.

Self-assessment questions:
- "Am I motivated to move mountains to protect and support businesses that depend on Stripe?"
- "Am I driven to overcome obstacles and take on big things to drive progress, or do I feel overwhelmed by the sheer scale and breadth of sometimes nebulous opportunities?"

### Trait 3: Thrive in Collaborative, Multidisciplinary Work

Working across technical, organizational, and geographic boundaries. Significant responsibilities at all levels of tenure and seniority.

Self-assessment questions:
- "Am I comfortable causing short-term friction in pursuit of better outcomes?"
- "Am I excited or anxious to be the first person to ever contemplate a particular problem?"

### Trait 4: Driven by Unbounded Opportunity

"You'll still hear us say 'we haven't won yet'..." — continuous growth mindset, not settling.

## 3. What "Users First" Means Concretely

### The Official Definition
> "We work backwards from our users' needs, indexing especially on feedback from the most innovative. Everyone at Stripe talks to users."

### CTO David Singleton's Emphasis
> "The most important operating principle is 'users first'" and engineers must "deeply understand users and work backwards from their needs."

### What It Looks Like in Practice

**Everyone talks to users.** Weekly all-hands meetings feature 20-minute customer testimonials. Teams hold open customer calls. In-app feedback, tweets, and signup notifications are funneled to Slack channels in real time to energize developers.

**Friction logging.** New team members conduct detailed product walkthroughs as user personas, documenting every imperfection to combat developer blindness to friction points.

**Overwhelming support for startups.** Senior leaders model this behavior. Teams stay connected to small merchants hitting their first revenue milestones alongside enterprise clients. The culture genuinely cares about the $5/month micro-merchant, not just the enterprise deal.

**Working backwards from the "Shipped email."** Teams sometimes begin a project by writing (but not sending) the shipped email — it serves as a north star for understanding scope and goals from the user's perspective.

**The TSE incarnation.** The TSE team is "the glue that supports developer-users and champions developer experience" and "interacts directly with developer-users more than any other team at Stripe." TSEs explain technical concepts, build and debug integrations alongside developers, produce/improve guides, identify points of friction, and collaborate with engineering teams to fix root causes.

### Behavioral Signals for "Users First"

**Hire signals:**
- Describes designing solutions starting from user needs, not technical convenience
- Proactively identified and fixed a user experience issue nobody asked about
- Has stories of talking directly to users and changing direction based on what they learned
- Demonstrates working backwards from the user's problem to the solution
- Shows genuine empathy for the developer reading docs at 11pm trying to debug an integration

**No-hire signals:**
- Talks exclusively about technical challenges without connecting to user impact
- Views user support as beneath engineering work
- Describes solutions in terms of architecture elegance without user context
- Cannot articulate who their users are or what they need

## 4. What "Curious" / "Stay Curious" Means at Stripe

### The Official Definitions

Short form:
> "We lead with a genuine interest in people, ideas, and the unknown. We work hard to understand other points of view, and prefer investigating to being right."

Long form:
> "Stripe is an applied exercise in learning about how businesses tick and how the world works. Since the details and possibilities are infinite, we are always seeking to learn. We're energized by the unfamiliar, preferring the joy of discovery to the comfort of certainty."

### What It Looks Like in Practice

**"Slope over y-intercept."** Stripe values individuals with steep learning curves and intellectual curiosity over those with narrow deep expertise. They favor smart generalists who can learn quickly rather than specialists limited by existing knowledge.

**Learning in public.** Engineers shepherd products across teams, documenting questions in "Project Brief" documents. Weekly learning segments, Slack Huddles for pairing, and document "mobbing" sessions.

**"Prefer investigating to being right."** This is the key phrase. Stripe wants people who will say "I don't know, let me find out" over people who bluff.

### Behavioral Signals for "Curious"

**Hire signals:**
- Has gone deep on something out of pure intrinsic motivation (not assigned by a manager)
- Changed their mind about something technical after learning new information — and can explain why
- Taught themselves a domain or technology and built something with it
- Asks thoughtful questions that reveal genuine interest (not performative questions)
- Career shows a pattern of seeking the unfamiliar

**No-hire signals:**
- Only learns what is directly required for the task at hand
- Cannot describe a time they changed their mind or approach
- Shows discomfort with ambiguity or unfamiliar territory
- Career shows comfort-seeking rather than growth-seeking

## 5. Patrick Collison and John Collison Quotes on Hiring and Culture

### Patrick Collison

**On the centrality of culture:**
> "You can't delegate culture."
> "When it comes to culture, I think the main mistakes that companies make are being too precious about it, being too apologetic about it, and not treating it as dynamic and subject to revision."

**On explicit cultural expectations:**
> "If you aren't having these explicit conversations about what your culture is, the downsides are threefold: You don't have the right people joining you, and you're being unfair to those who do join you, in the sense that they end up being surprised by this emergent friction and tension in work styles."

**On intensity and standards:**
> "We want people who really want to pour their hearts into this for several years, and we expect this to be the singular focus of your working life."
> "If you work with us, you're going to have to be okay with your work being repeatedly designated as inadequate, and okay with it being redone several times over."

**On hiring for happiness:**
> "We try to hire people who are intrinsically happy."
> Stripe screens for intrinsically happy people to avoid cultural toxicity and drain.

**On detail orientation:**
> "Because Stripe's domain is really complicated and the details really matter, if we make a mistake — just one mistake — there's a very good chance that somebody's paycheck is wrong... There's a culture at Stripe of just really prizing the small details."

**On being right vs. consistent:**
> "I think it's much better to be right than to be consistent."

**On failure acceptance:**
> Leaders should assume "70% of the things we try will turn out ex post facto to be mistakes."

**On craftsmanship:**
> "We talk a lot about craftsmanship and rigor and abstractions that can endure over decades and really getting those right. So I would say, precision is pretty deeply embedded."
> "We're all trying to impress upon people at Stripe the importance of multi-decadal abstractions."
> "When we think about introducing something new... Do we think we can stand behind this in 2044?"

**On mission:**
> "We're not here to build cars. We're here to build roads."

**On responsibility:**
> "We feel this extremely solemn and custodial responsibility to be able to provide uninterrupted service to them the way that they ought to expect."

**Three questions for leadership hires (from Inc.):**
1. "Are they capable of getting where you need to be in four years in two years?" — described as "a very high bar, and a lot of fabulous people will not be capable of that, but you're really short-changing your organization by not insisting on that."
2. Screens for people who are known to be good by others and attract other talent.
3. Focuses on ambition and pace — will they accelerate the organization?

**On optimism and paranoia:**
> "We're continually paranoid at the prospect that we might be forgetting something important."
> "We believe that Stripe will be far better in the future than it is today. When considering ideas, we think 'how might it work?' is more interesting than 'why will it fail?'"

### John Collison

**On hiring as branches of a tree:**
> "The correct way to view hiring is not, 'I am filling someone to do this role and fill this slot,' but rather 'The correct way to view hiring is branches of a tree.'"
> "When you hire this person, you're not only bringing them, but you're bringing their effect on the culture, and all the other people they're going to bring in with them, and the norms and the working style."

**On talent fixation:**
> "There has been no period in Stripe's history when we have not been fixated with hiring really talented people."

## 6. Stripe-Specific Cultural Norms

### Writing Culture

> "Stripe is a celebration of the written word which happens to be incorporated in the state of Delaware." — patio11 (kalzumeus)

Stripe has a writing-first culture. Patrick Collison values written communication because "writing enables better error detection and rigorous thinking over time compared to oral traditions."

Writing shows up everywhere:
- **Project Briefs:** Documents following templates of problem, context, solution, tasks, and metrics — circulated before work begins
- **Shipped Emails:** When a project ships, a detailed email summarizes the problem, context, solution approach (with screenshots/recordings), and quantifies impact with metrics
- **Strategic memos:** Accessible to all employees across all departments
- **Performance reviews** incorporate written peer recognition

### "We Haven't Won Yet"

Shipped emails "usually end with a 'we haven't won yet...' section where future phases or strategies may be laid out." This frames shipping as an ongoing journey, not a finish line. Projects sometimes begin by drafting the Shipped email to establish scope and goals.

The phrase became so iconic that The Information ran a story using it as a headline about Stripe's challenges during a period of growth.

### Micro-Pessimism, Macro-Optimism

Patrick Collison: "We want to be micro pessimists and macro optimists."

**Macro-optimistic:** Long-term belief that we can meaningfully do better — in progress, in technology, in economic enablement. Collison expresses certainty that "we could double our rate of progress." Cynicism is rejected. Problems are solvable with the right understanding.

**Micro-pessimistic:** Acutely aware of every shortcoming — clunky dashboards, broken APIs, inefficient navigation. Constantly thinking about what's broken, which problems lie around the corner, where unaddressed risks lie.

**Why both matter:** "This gives people structural permission to be extremely critical of that which exists today, because otherwise people feel a bit tentative."

To illustrate micro-pessimism: When Stripe moved offices, Collison wanted to display signs from failed companies — National Semiconductor, Atari, Osborne Computer, Sun Microsystems, SGI — as reminders that "Silicon Valley is famous for being a productive cradle for new technology companies, yet it's really the world's most densely populated graveyard."

### The DRI Model

Every project has a Directly Responsible Individual. "This helps to avoid the tragedy of the commons where shared ownership results in no ownership." Teams ask "who wants to DRI that idea?" during meetings.

### Gratitude Culture

Stripe built a `/++ @teammate` Slack feature for peer recognition, stored in employee profiles and used during performance reviews. "This practice normalizes gratitude in the team."

### Trust and Amplify

> "We want to work in a company of deeply good people who treat their colleagues exceptionally well. People should be committed to amplifying one another: to going out of their way to help each other in both the short- and long-term."

Stripe uses "See one, do one, teach one" — demonstrating how you want work done, having people do it themselves, then having them teach new people. "Trust saves a huge amount of time."

Decision-making is deliberately pushed down through the ranks. "As a founder or executive leader, you have to trust that others can make decisions in order to keep pushing the frontier of your business."

### Think Rigorously

> "We care about getting things right and it often takes reasoning from first principles to get there. We work hard to detect the errors in received wisdom. Rigor doesn't mean not-invented-here syndrome; we're interested in the world around us and think that other companies, industries, and academic fields have a lot to teach us."

### Global Optimization

Think internationally given Stripe's diverse workforce and mission to increase internet GDP globally. No local optimization at the expense of the whole.

### "Nothing is Not My Job"

Stripe's cultural ethos encourages employees to contribute outside formal responsibilities. patio11's first pull request at Stripe was fixing a security bug — from someone who was hired for Atlas, not security.

### Data Accessibility

Unlike many companies, Stripe opens its data warehouse to all employees with appropriate access controls, enabling teams to analyze information relevant to their roles rather than queuing requests from a data team.

## 7. What Makes TSE Different from SWE at Stripe

### The Official Description

> "The Technical Solutions Engineering team is the glue that supports developer-users and champions developer experience as being central to the overall experience of Stripe's users. TSEs interact directly with developer-users more than any other team at Stripe."

### Key Differences

| Dimension | SWE | TSE |
|---|---|---|
| Primary focus | Building products and systems | Supporting developer-users and championing DX |
| Customer interaction | Indirect (through product) | Direct (daily, across all skill levels) |
| Code work | Building features, shipping infrastructure | Building internal tools, writing demos, debugging integrations, reading others' code |
| Feedback loop | Metrics, A/B tests | Direct user conversations, support tickets |
| Cross-functional | Eng ↔ Eng | Eng ↔ Support ↔ Product ↔ Docs ↔ Users |
| Advocacy role | Internal to engineering | Developer advocate both internally and externally |

### TSE-Specific Responsibilities

- Explain technical concepts about Stripe's products to developers of all skill levels
- Build and debug integrations alongside developers
- Produce and improve guides, documentation, code snippets
- Identify points of friction developers encounter and collaborate with engineering teams to fix root causes
- Participate in extended engineering teams' planning sessions, design reviews, and development meetings
- Act as a developer advocate and educational resource internally
- Develop and maintain internal support tooling (including RAG-based LLM Copilot tools)
- Assemble training for non-developers and respond in real-time to product integration questions

### Unique Qualities That Matter for TSE

1. **Developer empathy** — You've been the developer on the receiving end. You know what good DX feels like.
2. **Polyglot comfort** — "TSEs understand the fundamentals of programming incredibly well and eagerly jump in and familiarize themselves with others' code." You read code in languages you don't primarily write.
3. **Communication breadth** — You explain technical concepts to both technical and non-technical audiences.
4. **Debugging in someone else's context** — Not just debugging your own code, but helping someone debug their integration with Stripe's API.
5. **Proactive friction-finding** — You don't wait for bug reports; you find the friction points yourself.
6. **Writing** — Docs, guides, code samples, internal knowledge. The writing culture matters double for TSE.
7. **Enthusiasm for helping others succeed** — This is the most important one. The job is fundamentally about making other developers successful.

## 8. Behavioral Interview Rubric and Signals

### Interview Structure for TSE

The typical TSE onsite includes:
- 1-2 coding/programming exercises (paired, screen-shared)
- 1 integration exercise (work in existing codebase, HTTP/API, testing)
- 1-2 behavioral rounds (Experience & Goals; Users First & Curious)

The behavioral round is typically 45-60 minutes, led by a hiring manager or "Leveler" (Stripe's equivalent of Amazon's Bar Raiser — someone who interviews across levels to maintain a consistent bar).

### How Decisions Are Made

- All onsite panelists submit **written feedback** after interviews
- The panel meets to discuss findings
- Most decisions reach **consensus** (unanimous hire/no-hire)
- When consensus fails, the **hiring manager has final say**
- Behavioral evaluation is **woven into every round**, not confined to a single interview

### Cultural Alignment Is a Hard Filter

> "At Stripe, cultural alignment is a hard filter, not a tiebreaker. Candidates who ace every technical round but show poor self-awareness, ego, or inability to collaborate are rejected."

### The Leveling Factor

The behavioral round is "particularly important for leveling — if candidates can't effectively communicate the business impact of their work, for instance, they may be down-leveled."

One candidate on Levels.fyi reportedly "aced all 4 technical rounds but failed Experience & Goals due to 'lack of signal in highlighting large impact projects with large number of contributors.'"

### Positive Behavioral Signals (Hire)

- **Ownership:** Takes responsibility for outcomes, not just tasks. DRIs their stories.
- **Clear communication:** Explains complex technical decisions without losing precision. Structured, concise, written-communication-quality verbal answers.
- **Intellectual honesty:** Says "I don't know, but here's how I would figure it out" rather than bluffing. Admits uncertainty.
- **Humility:** Gives credit to teammates. Builds on others' ideas. Acknowledges mistakes.
- **Impact quantification:** Has numbers. Knows the business impact of their work.
- **Developer empathy:** Instinctively considers "Does this make the developer's life easier or harder?"
- **Cross-functional collaboration:** Stories involve working with multiple teams, partners, stakeholders.
- **Learning and growth:** Reflects on what they learned. Shows evolution in thinking.
- **Proactivity:** Identified problems nobody asked them to solve. Built things that enabled others.
- **STAR stories that end with reflection:** STAR + R (Retrospective). "What I learned" or "What I'd do differently."

### Negative Behavioral Signals (No-Hire)

- **Poor self-awareness or ego-driven behavior** — talks about "I" exclusively, doesn't credit teammates
- **Inability to collaborate cross-functionally** — all stories are solo work
- **Rambling, unstructured communication** — can't tell a story in under 2 minutes
- **Over-engineering or unnecessary complexity** — confuses complicated with good
- **Treating behavioral rounds as secondary** — clearly didn't prepare, gives generic answers
- **Bluffing instead of admitting uncertainty** — contradicts the "prefer investigating to being right" principle
- **Negativity about past employers or teammates** — contradicts the exothermic and humble principles
- **Cannot articulate user impact** — talks about technology without connecting to who it serves
- **No evidence of curiosity or learning** — career shows comfort-seeking, not growth-seeking

### Specific Behavioral Questions Reported by Candidates

**Experience & Goals round:**
- "Describe a technically challenging project and how you approached it"
- "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a team decision. What did you do?"
- "Tell me about a time you made a mistake. What did you learn?"
- "What's the hardest bug you've ever debugged?"
- "What are you looking for in your next role?"
- "Tell me about a technical contribution that allowed others to be successful"
- "How do you handle deadlines?"
- "How do you break down tasks?"
- "How do you collaborate with teammates during unclear or changing requirements?"

**Users First & Curious round:**
- "Tell me about a time you designed a solution starting from the user's perspective"
- "Tell me about a time you proactively identified a UX issue nobody asked you to fix"
- "Tell me about a time you went deep on something out of pure curiosity"
- "Describe a time you changed your mind after learning new information"
- "Tell me about a time you adjusted your approach based on new insights"

**General behavioral (any round):**
- "Describe a time you worked on a team project. What was your role, and how did you handle conflicts?"
- "Can you share an example of a challenging problem you faced and how you approached it?"
- "Tell me about a time when you had to adapt to a significant change at work"
- "Give an example of how you've prioritized customer needs in your work"
- "Describe a time you made a mistake at work. What did you learn from it?"

## 9. What "Exothermic" and "Macro-Optimistic" Mean

### Exothermic

> "Stripes generate an energy and warmth that is infectious across teams and throughout the company. We are genuinely excited about our work, and about creating an exceptionally welcoming environment for all Stripes."

The metaphor is from chemistry: an exothermic reaction **releases** energy. Stripe wants people who radiate positive energy to those around them, not people who absorb energy from the room.

Connected concepts:
- Patrick Collison screens for "intrinsically happy" people
- The `/++ @teammate` Slack gratitude feature normalizes positive recognition
- "Mad Props" segments in team wrap-ups
- Stripe's culture of positivity is intentional and structural, not just lip service
- If Slack conversations become negative, someone publicly redirects them toward "a more constructive tone"

**What this means for interviews:** Bring genuine enthusiasm. Talk about what excites you about the work, not just what you've accomplished. Show warmth toward your teammates in your stories. Be the kind of person the interviewer would want to work with every day.

### Macro-Optimistic

> "Stripes reject cynicism, knowing that all problems can be solved with the right understanding, and that progress is only inevitable through focused effort."

Patrick Collison's formulation: "We want to be micro pessimists and macro optimists."

- **Macro:** Believe the future will be better than the present. Economic growth is the most important development in human history. The internet economy's potential is vastly underexplored. Progress is possible but not guaranteed — it requires focused effort.
- **Micro:** Be rigorously critical of what exists today. Find every bug, every friction point, every shortcoming. Don't confuse optimism with complacency.

**What this means for interviews:** When discussing challenges, show that you approached them with a belief that the problem was solvable. Don't be cynical about past employers. Frame failures as learning opportunities, not evidence that things are broken beyond repair.

## 10. The "Stripe Quick Culture Guide" (Blake Irving / early internal doc)

Key principles from the early internal culture guide that still resonate:

1. **Incomplete mission.** "You will, within weeks of joining Stripe, work on problems that no one here has solved before."
2. **Urgency with responsibility.** "Millions of businesses around the world are open for business only if we are."
3. **Rigorous thinking.** First-principles reasoning over conventional wisdom. Actively seek opposing viewpoints.
4. **Trust-based environment.** Not a "no asshole rule" — they seek "deeply good people who treat their colleagues exceptionally well."
5. **Organization over individual.** "No bonus points for building large teams." People routinely contribute across departments.
6. **Measured judgment.** "Stripes don't yell" despite engaging in strenuous discussions.
7. **Macro optimism, micro pessimism.** (See above.)
8. **Neutrality on customers.** Stripe processes payments for diverse political candidates and values, believing financial infrastructure access matters regardless of customer ideology.
